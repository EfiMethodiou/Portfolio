Rhizone: PapiLLa
Immersive, multisensory VR environment
2020

RHIZONE: [PapiLLa] explores in a poetic way the essence of human identity in the world where organic and inorganic systems will merge into a new hybrid whole of Big Data processing. What will happen when the Internet of Everything will turn into the Internet of Minds? Inspired by symbiotic natural systems of Wood Wide Web the multi-sensual poetic narrative immerses the viewer in a complex, multi-dimensional semi-biological information network. Inside, the boundaries between micro and macro systems, between "internal" and "external" data processing are blurred.
The piece is an immersive installation combining VR with a sensory environment. The title refers to biological nodular formations often associated with the senses of touch, taste or smell, as well as to the organs of symbiosis between plants and bacteria produced by plants on their roots.

PapiLLa
It is a fractal of a virtual symbiote-system (EpiMimesis) It is its sensory organ, a hybrid space in which the viewer's feelings and imagination co-create the invigorating web of sympoiesis.
The elusive threads of internal imagination, like invasive threads of Rhizobium, connect a participant with a structure devoid of spatial and temporal boundaries.

artist Joanna Hoffmann

Professor at the University of Arts in Poznań, leader of the Studio for Transdisciplinary Projects & Research. She is co-founder of the Art & Science Synergy Foundation and Chair of the Art & Science Node in Berlin as well as co-leader of the Club of Science and Art in Poznań. She works in the field of multimedia installation, XR, experimental video-animation, interactive projects, and book art. Her artistic works have been widely presented in many exhibitions and festivals i.a. at the DANA Centre/Science Museum and MOCA Museum of Contemporary Art in London, Transmediale Festival for Digital Culture in Berlin, MUSE Centre of Photography and the Moving Image in New York, and the Hiroshima Prefectural Art Museum in Japan.

Living Systems

By exploring the richness of the relations in healthy ecosystems, we find abundance, great efficiency and resilience. At the same time, on many occasions we can clearly see the connections between man and its environment being fragmented, if not lost. ‘Broken Links’ by Maria Subczyńska and ‘Regnum animale’ by Larys Lubowicki question the anthropocentric attitude and underline the importance of maintaining the ecosystems’ equilibrium and reconnecting with their networks. While the artists refer to fungi and wild animals, ‘Virophilia’ by Pei-Ying Lin explores the invisible world of viruses, which despite their importance and omnipresence, elude our perception. ‘The Law of Relationship & Interdependence’ – a performance by Małgorzata Kaczmarek, underlines the fact that the various forms of symbiotic relations and processes are indispensable for our habitat to exist.

Tracing Links

To express the complexity of our dynamic environment, artists experiment with various tools and strategies. In a short, elegant animation titled ‘Morphism’, Marta Bączyk retraces silhouettes of her own relatives, creating the mycelium-like entanglements, in which an individual being cannot be distinguished from the others. Retracing also relates to the passing time. In the ‘Algorithmic Photography’ series, Alex May captures the unnoticed patterns of movements in our environment. revealing them with the use of a specially designed algorithm. Similarly, an original code guides two other artworks – ‘Contactless Root Interface’ by Piotr Słomczewski and ‘defined_not’ by Diana Taukin. The former uses a metaphor of the root to draw connections between the virtual and physical realities. The latter underlines the importance of nature-based inspirations, using biomimicry as a tool for perfecting and diversifying human design.

Shifting Horizons

Speculation over the possible future(s) offers various perspectives. In one of them, after destroying the natural habitats, people drift away into oblivion. In ‘Drifting Particles’ by Jadwiga Subczyńska an intelligent substance takes over the post-apocalyptic reality, often taking anthropomorphic shapes. In this gesture, the artist ironically comments on the condition of our connection to nature. In turn, Paulina Misiak, the author of ‘CALL IN’ series, presents different scales and viewpoints, inviting us to go beyond our daily, centrifugal perception.

Another perspective enforces the interconnectivity of various layers of reality, merging them in a vision of one, hybrid whole, that has the potential to function better than the sum of its elements. ‘Vitruvian WoMan’ by Joanna Hoffmann, with sound by Andre Bartetzki is a statement of such hybrid reality, offering the positive reflection over the growing common awareness of us as ‘creative participants of Nature’. In ‘Marsquakes’ by Jadwiga Subczyńska the idea of digital plants ‘living on Mars’ serves an optimistic vision of transferring human needs for existing with nature to another level. Artist envisions that the colonization of Mars may be viewed not necessarily as an escape or abandonment, but as a quest for new qualities.

The above visions and pathways enforce the necessity to actively take care of the present day, for the benefit of the future.

Hybrid Realities

Our universe is built up out of an enormous number of linkages made on every possible scale, of dynamic, interactive networks of constantly exchanging data. From the Millenium Simulation of the universe's structure, through the inter-connection of global ecological systems, to the neural networks and world wide web – we can see that our digital and technological networks and strategies have more in common with the physical and biological evolutionary processes we can grasp.

Even by observing ourselves, we discover the complexity of data and structures interactions. Taking an example of a sensory perception system, 'Aftertaste' by Jill Scott & Marille Hahne reveals how we are constantly and incessantly fusing with the outside world at the molecular level. Being one of the projects implemented as part of the CHIC ASN Artists-in-Residence Program, 'Aftertaste' shows how molecules obtained from the chicory root influence our gustatory and olfactory systems. The second work of CHIC ASN A-I-R Program – 'Biotechnology from the Blue Flower' by Anna Dumitriu & Alex May refers to the chicory’s blue flower and its symbolic meaning. The human perspective of understanding the natural world is translated here into the cultural heritage that unites us with the environment. Recalling the time of Goethe and industrial revolution, the artwork takes upon the topic of the ongoing, biotechnological one. Today’s transformations and biotechnological trends are also a theme of 'Rhizone: PapiLLa' by Joanna Hoffmann, with soundscape by Andre Bartetzki. The artwork, inspired by the rhizosphere and relation between plants and the symbiotic, nitrogen-fixing bacteria occurring in the root nodules, emphasizes the complexity and entanglement of biological and technological structures.
